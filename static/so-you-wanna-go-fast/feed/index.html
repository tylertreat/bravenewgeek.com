]]> By: Randall https://bravenewgeek.com/so-you-wanna-go-fast/#comment-14068 Thu, 25 Feb 2016 03:12:50 +0000 https://bravenewgeek.com/so-you-wanna-go-fast/#comment-14068 On the channels section, “most multithreaded apps should use nonblocking ring buffers” is substantially the wrong message for most folks, who are dealing with something like the 3µs per channel send/receive pair you measure in one benchmark (or less). If 3µs/communication is too much, as a rule you’ve already lost.

Looking at code out on the Web, other Go users’ discussions, etc, it seems like two of the key things people need to know are 1) you don’t use channels (vs. locks, atomics, etc.) for everything, and 2) you have to design around minimizing the total amount of coordination.

Introductory materials are actively confusing on point 1, I think in the name of demonstrating language features to newbies. I certainly fell victim to it, and I’m pretty sure my first app in effect rebuilt locks and maybe semaphores, inefficiently, using channels. You should start out using whatever primitive maps best to your intent, and revise based on perf as needed; channels aren’t always the right thing.

On point 2, I routinely people see people confused because it’s not helping their app to send tiny tiny tasks over a channel when using larger work chunks (or, sometimes, just adding a channel buffer) would solve their problem. It’s not really hard, usually, but it does seem to be rarely discussed.

If there is read-write contention at the level seen in your benchmark, and you can’t prevent coordination overhead from taking up a bunch of your app’s CPU time, then there’s certainly a case for using a ring buffer or such. (Go doesn’t use one by default because the spec says first-in-first-out and I think lock-free solutions tend to weaken those guarantees. Could be wrong.) Again, though, the modal application of channels seems to be a vanilla work queue or signalling setup, hence “wrong message for most folks.” Pick the most natural primitive (including using what the language gave you), do a few basic things to cut overhead, then worry more if you turn out to need to.

In support of the notion that you can use channels for fast code, I’d note that https://github.com/twotwotwo/sorts uses channels and puts up some decent #s for parallel sort (these are on an old v of Go, but see https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1GkXMLXQ7oW5Bp0qwyYw0IiQElIq8B-IvNEYE_RPCTvA/edit#). I know you guys have tackled parallel sorting at https://github.com/Workiva/go-datastructures/tree/master/sort — would actively welcome being one-upped here. :)
